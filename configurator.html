Configurator products

Your "build your own" products: how they work, how to safely change options and prices, and how to build a new one. This is the most powerful part of your store, so it is worth reading this page fully before you change anything.

What a configurator product is

Most products in your store are fixed: one item, one price. A configurator product is different. It starts from a base price and then offers the customer a set of option menus, for example hose length, end fittings, and colour. Each choice can add to the price and adds a code to a part number that describes exactly what was built. When the customer adds it to the cart, they are ordering that specific configuration.

A good example is the Oxygen Hose product. Its base price is $16.00, and it offers six option menus. As the shopper chooses a length and fittings, the price and the picture update, and a part number such as OH-120CD01 is assembled behind the scenes.

Good to know

Configurator products are also called composite products, because the final item is composed from several choices. In the product list they carry the tags configurable and composite, and a setting called is_configurable is switched on.

The one thing to understand first: menus are shared

This is the single most important idea on this page. The option menus are not stored inside each product. They are stored once, in a shared library, and many products point at the same menus. In Shopify this kind of shared, reusable content is called a metaobject.

Your library contains:

Building blockWhat it isHow many you have
Option GroupOne menu of choices, for example "Hose Length"179
Option ChoiceOne selectable option inside a group, for example "10 Feet"1,574
Take care

Because these menus are shared, editing one choice can change every product that uses it. If you rename or reprice a choice in a group that several hoses share, all of those hoses change at once. This is usually what you want, but always pause and ask: "which products use this menu?" before editing.

Do not

Never delete an option group or option choice unless you are certain no product uses it. Deleting a shared choice removes it from every product at once and cannot be undone. To take a choice out of circulation safely, hide it instead (explained below).

How a configurator product is put together

Each configurator product is a normal Shopify product with some extra fields that turn it into a builder. Here are the pieces, using the Oxygen Hose as the example.

The product and its base priceproduct

The product has a single variant that holds the starting price. On the Oxygen Hose that variant is OH-BASE at $16.00. Every option the customer adds is added on top of this base.

custom.is_configurabletrue / false

The switch that tells the store to show the builder instead of a normal buy button. On configurator products this is set to true.

custom.sku_prefixtext

The starting code for the built part number. On the Oxygen Hose it is OH-. The chosen option codes are added after this prefix.

custom.option_groupslist of option groups

The ordered list of option menus this product shows. The Oxygen Hose points at six option groups. Changing this list changes which menus appear on the product, and in what order.

custom.cfg_price_mappricing table

A behind-the-scenes table the product page uses to total the build instantly as the customer clicks. It mirrors the prices held on the choices. See The price map below, because this is the one thing that needs care when you change prices.

custom.gas_grouplist of products

The related products offered by the gas-type switcher on the page. See The gas-type switcher.

custom.cfg_stack_baseimage

The base picture that the option image layers sit on top of. See The layered product image.

Finding the products and the option menus

Find a configurator product

  1. Open your products. Go to Products›All products
  2. Search by name. Type the product name, for example "Oxygen Hose", and click it to open it.
  3. Scroll to the extra fields. The configurator settings (option_groups, sku_prefix, and so on) appear in the Metafields area lower down the product page.

Find the shared option menus

  1. Open Metaobjects. Go to Content›Metaobjects
  2. Choose the type. Click Option Group to see all 179 menus, or Option Choice to see all 1,574 individual options.
  3. Open one to edit it. Use the search box to find a group by name, for example "Hose Length", and click it to open its fields.
Good to know

You can edit an option menu either from the Metaobjects area, or directly from within a product where it is used. Both open the same shared menu, so a change made in one place shows everywhere that menu is used.

Inside an option group

An option group is one menu, such as "Hose Length". When you open a group you will see these fields.

display_nametext

The heading the customer sees above the menu, for example Hose Length. Safe to reword, but remember it may appear on several products.

ui_typetext

How the menu is shown, for example simple-dropdown. Leave this unless your developer advises a change.

is_requiredtrue / false

Whether the customer must make a choice from this menu before adding to cart.

part_number_positionnumber

Where this group's code sits in the assembled part number. Position 1 comes first, then 2, and so on. See How the part number is built.

part_number_paddingnumber

Pads a group's codes to a fixed length. On Hose Length the padding is 3, so the code for 36 inches is written as 036.

default_choiceone option choice

The choice that is pre-selected when the product first loads.

choiceslist of option choices

The options in this menu, in order. This is where you add, remove, or reorder the choices shown.

layer_znumber

The stacking order of this group's image layer in the product picture. See The layered product image.

Inside an option choice

An option choice is a single selectable option, such as "120 Inches (10 Feet)". When you open a choice you will see these fields. The example values are from that real choice.

display_nametext

The label the customer reads, for example 120 Inches (10 Feet).

part_codetext

The short code this choice adds to the part number, for example 120.

base_price_deltamoney

The amount this choice adds to (or, if negative, subtracts from) the base price. For the 10 foot length it is $34.00. This is the source of truth for the choice's price.

sort_ordernumber

Where the choice appears in the menu. Lower numbers appear first.

is_hiddentrue / false

Set to true to hide a choice from customers without deleting it. This is the safe way to retire an option.

belongs_to_groupone option group

Which menu this choice belongs to. Leave this as set.

layer_imageimage

The picture layer shown for this choice on products that use layered images. See The layered product image.

Common edits, step by step

Rename a choice or a menu heading

  1. Open the item. Find the option choice (or option group) in Content›Metaobjects
  2. Edit the wording. Change the display_name field.
  3. Save. The new wording shows on every product that uses it.
Do

Renaming for clarity or to fix a typo is safe. It changes only the label, not the price or the part number.

Hide a choice you no longer offer

  1. Open the option choice in the Metaobjects area.
  2. Set is_hidden to true. Tick or switch on the is_hidden field.
  3. Save. The choice disappears from the storefront but its record and any past orders are kept.
Good to know

Always prefer hiding over deleting. Hidden choices can be brought back at any time, and they do not disturb the other choices in the menu.

Change which choice is selected by default

  1. Open the option group (the menu), not the choice.
  2. Set default_choice. Pick the choice you want pre-selected in the default_choice field.
  3. Save.

Reorder the choices in a menu

  1. Open each choice you want to move.
  2. Adjust sort_order. Lower numbers appear higher in the list. Space your numbers out (10, 20, 30) so you can slot items in later.
  3. Save.

Change the price of a choice

  1. Open the option choice in the Metaobjects area.
  2. Edit base_price_delta. Enter the new amount this choice adds to the base price.
  3. Save.
  4. Ask for a price refresh. Have your developer regenerate the price map on the affected products (explained next), so the live total matches the new price.
Take care

A choice's price is stored in two places: on the choice itself (base_price_delta) and in each product's fast pricing table (cfg_price_map). If you change one without the other, the page can show a different total from what you intended. Always pair a price change with a price-map refresh. See below.

Add a new choice to a menu

  1. Create the choice. Go to Content›Metaobjects›Option Choice and click Add entry.
  2. Fill the fields. Set display_name, part_code, base_price_delta, sort_order, and set belongs_to_group to the correct menu.
  3. Add it to the menu. Open the option group and add the new choice to its choices list.
  4. Save, then request a price-map refresh for the products that use this menu.

Change which menus appear on a product

  1. Open the product in Products and scroll to its Metafields.
  2. Edit option_groups. Add or remove option groups from the custom.option_groups list, and drag them into the order you want them shown.
  3. Save, then request a price-map refresh for that product.

How the part number is built

When a customer finishes a build, the store assembles a part number so your team knows exactly what was ordered. The recipe is:

The recipe

sku_prefix first, then each menu's chosen part_code added in part_number_position order (1, then 2, then 3), with each code padded to the length set by part_number_padding.

For example, on the Oxygen Hose (sku_prefix = OH-), choosing the 10 foot length (position 1, code 120) followed by the codes from the other menus produces a part number that begins OH-120... and continues with each further choice.

Take care

Part codes and positions must be correct, or the assembled part number will be wrong on the order. When you add a choice, double check its part_code, and avoid starting a code with a symbol such as a plus or minus sign, because that can be misread. If in doubt, copy the pattern of an existing choice in the same menu.

The price map, and why prices need a refresh

To make the total update the instant a customer clicks, each configurator product carries a small pricing table called custom.cfg_price_map. It lists the base price and every choice's price, so the page never has to pause and calculate. It is a copy of the choice prices, kept for speed.

Do not

Do not hand-edit the cfg_price_map field. It is generated automatically and holds prices in a coded format. Editing it by hand can make the displayed total wrong.

Do

When you change any choice price, edit the choice's base_price_delta (the source of truth), then ask your developer to regenerate the price map for the affected products. This keeps the shown total and the real price in step.

The layered product image

On configurator products, the picture is built by stacking transparent image layers, so it can reflect the chosen options. There is a base image (custom.cfg_stack_base), and each option group has a layer order (layer_z) that decides which layers sit on top. A choice can carry its own layer image (layer_image) that appears when it is selected.

  1. To replace an image layer, open the option choice (for a per-choice layer) or the product (for the base image) and swap the image field.
  2. Match the size and alignment. The new image must be the same dimensions and positioned the same way as the one it replaces, so the layers line up.
  3. Save and preview the product on the storefront to confirm the stack still lines up.
Take care

The layers are prepared as matching, full-size images that align pixel for pixel. A new image that is a different size or crop will look misaligned. For new layer artwork, work with your designer or developer so the alignment is kept.

The gas-type switcher

Many hose and regulator products let a shopper switch the gas type (for example Oxygen, Air, Vacuum, Nitrogen, Nitrous Oxide, or CO2) right on the product page and jump to the matching product. This is powered by custom.gas_group, a list on each product naming its sibling products.

Take care

For the switcher to work both ways, every product in a gas family should list all the others. If you add a new gas variation, ask your developer to update the whole family so each product points to the others. This feature is also covered on the Store features page.

Building a brand-new configurator product

This is the most advanced task in your store. You can do it yourself once you are comfortable with the pieces above, but for a first build it is best done alongside your developer.

Take care

A configurator product only works correctly when the product settings, the option menus, the part number codes, and the price map all agree. The final two steps below (the price map, and testing) are where a developer's help matters most.

  1. Create the product. In Products›Add product add the title, description, and photos, and set the single variant's price to the base price. Keep it Draft while you build.
  2. Turn it into a configurator. In the Metafields, set custom.is_configurable to true and set custom.sku_prefix to the product's starting code.
  3. Choose its option menus. Reuse existing option groups where you can, or create new ones with their choices, then list them in custom.option_groups in the order they should appear.
  4. Check codes and positions. Make sure each group has the right part_number_position and each choice has the right part_code.
  5. Generate the price map. Have your developer create custom.cfg_price_map for the product so totals update live.
  6. Set up images if the product uses layered pictures (base image plus layer images).
  7. Preview and test. View the product on the storefront, run through the options, and confirm the price, the picture, and the part number all look right.
  8. Go live. When everything checks out, set the product to Active.
Remember

The option menus are shared, prices live in two places that must stay in step, and part numbers depend on exact codes. When in doubt on a configurator product, duplicate to a draft to practise, or ask your developer. Everyday jobs like renaming, hiding a choice, or changing a default are safe to do yourself.
